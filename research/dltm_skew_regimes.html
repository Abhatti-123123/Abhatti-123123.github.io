From Trend-Following to Skew Prediction: Empirical Applications of the dLTM Long-Memory Estimator

Two empirical tests of whether dLTM regimes carry tradeable information. A sector-balanced 30-name S&P 500 trend portfolio with memory-conditioned sizing produces statistically significant Sharpe lifts on both binary and continuous trend specifications. On a panel of four optionable underlyings, forward realized skewness is monotone in dLTM regime and survives controls for Bakshi-Kapadia-Madan implied skew.

Background and motivation

The dLTM estimator from Bhatti (2026a) recovers a finite-sample long-memory parameter from the tail slope of the autocorrelation function of log prices. The companion paper on over-differencing shows on a panel of 484 S&P 500 names that realized return skewness deepens from about -0.14 to -0.46 as one moves from the low-dLTM regime (d < 0.3) to the high-dLTM regime (d ≥ 0.7). The mechanism is mechanical. More persistent return processes accumulate negative shocks inside trending regimes, which concentrates left-tail mass.

The natural follow-up question is whether that regime-conditional skew prediction has practical applications in equity portfolio construction or options trading. This note presents two empirical tests addressing that question.

Application 1: trend-following with memory-conditioned sizing

Setup

A sector-balanced 30-name S&P 500 universe is selected by long history (15+ years) and dLTM coverage (50%+ valid estimates), with three names per sector across ten sectors. The base strategy is a Carver-style risk-scaled portfolio with two trend overlay variants: a binary mask (Strategy 5) and a continuous risk-adjusted forecast (Strategy 7). For each variant, an overlay scales position size by current dLTM regime. Positions in high-d names are scaled up by conf_max = 1.5, positions in low-d names are scaled down by conf_min = 0.5, and mid-d positions are unchanged. Regime cutoffs (d_low = 0.3, d_high = 0.7) match the Paper 2 buckets. All parameters are fixed ex ante. The backtest spans 2000-2025; dLTM walk-forward estimates begin July 2009 after a 2,400-day warmup. Standard errors are HAC. Sharpe differentials are tested via Diebold-Mariano on the daily return differential with Newey-West bandwidth of 21.

Interpretation

The overlay performs as expected if dLTM is a tradeable regime indicator for conditional skew, since trend-following is naturally short-skew at the position level. Scaling up in high-dLTM regimes captures additional trend premium during periods where the underlying is in a high-memory state, which empirically coincides with stronger directional persistence. The cost is moderately deeper drawdowns in periods where the high-d state coincides with stress rather than continuation. This is consistent with skew-premium harvesting. The lift reflects accepting larger left-tail exposure in exchange for higher unconditional return.

A note on the inverse direction

I separately tested the inverse overlay (high-d to smaller positions), motivated by the intu intuition that one could hedge the predicted negative skew by reducing exposure. The result is not statistically meaningful (strength: lift +0.08, p = 0.11; mask: lift -0.02, not significant), and maximum drawdown deteriorates in stress regimes (e.g. COVID crash worsens from -14% to -32%).

This is a feature of the construction, not a bug. Shrinking a long equity position is not a tail hedge, since the smaller position is still long the same risk. Both directions of the dLTM signal carry economic content (negative skew can be harvested as a premium or hedged as a risk), but the hedge interpretation requires a tail-protective instrument such as long puts, collars, or VIX exposure sized by the regime indicator, not a position-size dampener on the underlying. I retain the aggressive direction here and reserve the defensive application for future work using options instruments.

Application 2: skew prediction on optionable underlyings

If the Section 2 result is driven by the predicted skew mechanism, then dLTM should also predict realized skew directly on individual underlyings, and ideally beyond what the options market already prices via implied skew. This section tests that claim using OptionsDX end-of-day chains for four liquid optionable names.

What did not replicate (options test)

Three caveats apply to the options test and should be stated plainly.

(i) Per-name precision. Zero of four names individually pass a pre-committed t < -1.5 threshold on the d_high coefficient. The result is detectable at the cross-sectional level but not at the single-name level. This is consistent with the mechanism living in cross-sectional differences, where Paper 2 originally documented it, rather than in individual-name time-series predictability.

(ii) Subperiod stability on SPY. Splitting the SPY sample at 2017-01-01, the linear-specification dLTM coefficient is -0.46 (t=-1.05) pre-2017 and -0.26 (t=-0.13) post-2017. The full-sample t-statistic on linear specification (-2.27) reflects pooling, not stable predictability across time.

(iii) Regime imbalance. The 2016-2023 sample sits almost entirely in high-dLTM territory for mega-cap technology names. Inference about low-d regimes from this sub-panel is structurally limited.

Despite these caveats, the result is consistent with the Carver application: dLTM-conditioned skew effects are real but require cross-sectional aggregation (across names or across regimes) to be detected with high confidence. Single-name, single-time-series applications are too noisy.

Strategy implications

Taking the two applications together, two strategies are potentially viable.

(A) dLTM-aggressive trend overlay (equity). Direct replication of Application 1. Statistically significant Sharpe lift on a 30-name S&P 500 trend portfolio, on both binary and continuous trend specifications. Mechanism is skew-premium harvesting: positions are scaled up in regimes predicted to have more-negative future skew, capturing additional return in exchange for somewhat deeper drawdowns. Suitable as an overlay rule in a multi-rule trend book.

(B) Cross-sectional regime-contrast skew portfolio (options). Long realized-skew exposure on low-d names (long OTM put plus short OTM call, delta-hedged), short realized-skew exposure on high-d names. Equal-vega sizing, 30-day tenor rolling weekly. The trade is structurally a long-short factor portfolio that exploits the pooled regime contrast shown in the regime-ordering table, not a single-name skew bet. Expected gross Sharpe before transaction costs is in the 0.4 to 0.7 range; net Sharpe after realistic OTM single-name options bid-ask costs (0.5% to 1.5% per round trip) is uncertain.

Construction of (B) requires a larger underlying universe (20 to 30 names) than the four tested here. Expanding the underlying panel is the natural next step and the gating decision for whether the options strategy is worth full backtest construction. A separate extension involves applying dLTM as a sizing signal on a tail-protective options overlay (long puts, collars, VIX longs) rather than on the underlying equity position. The negative result in the defensive test reflects the wrong instrument, not the wrong signal.
