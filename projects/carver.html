Systematic Futures Portfolio Construction

A Carver-style implementation focused on futures back-adjustment, volatility targeting, risk parity, covariance estimation, trend overlays, leverage, and drawdown-aware strategy evaluation.

Objective

This project implements systematic futures trading strategies inspired by Robert Carver's framework. The goal was not simply to maximize backtest return. The goal was to understand how data construction, volatility estimation, position sizing, diversification, leverage, and trend filters interact to shape Sharpe ratio, drawdowns, tail behavior, and robustness across regimes.

Data construction and futures adjustment

A key implementation issue is that futures prices must be back-adjusted correctly. I used continuous back-adjusted futures logic, including the Panama-style adjustment, so that roll gaps, dividends, and interest-related effects are not incorrectly treated as trading losses. The point is to measure the economic return from holding the futures strategy, not artificial jumps caused by contract rolls.

I also treated instrument-specific contract mechanics as part of the risk system. Riskier futures generally have higher initial margin requirements, require more cash to start trading, and can create larger variation margin calls once held. This matters because position sizing cannot be separated from contract notional, capital, margin, and leverage.

Risk metrics and evaluation principles

I evaluated strategies using both Sharpe and Sortino ratios. Sharpe subtracts the risk-free rate and divides by total volatility, so it penalizes both upside and downside volatility. Sortino is useful as a complement because it focuses on downside volatility and helps detect negative skew.

I treated drawdown analysis as central. Maximum drawdown is useful, but for fat-tailed returns, average drawdown and tail metrics are often more informative than one realized worst event. Since strategy returns are fat-tailed, lower- and upper-percentile ratios are more useful than relying on kurtosis alone.

Volatility estimation

Equal-weighted rolling standard deviation can create fake volatility jumps. If a large return occurred 28 days ago, volatility can suddenly fall or jump when that observation exits the rolling window, even though the current risk environment has not changed. To avoid this artifact, I used exponentially weighted volatility estimates.

The logic is that volatility clusters in the short run but mean reverts over longer horizons. For forecasting future volatility, I therefore blend short-run and long-run volatility estimates. The short-run estimate reacts to current market stress; the long-run estimate prevents the strategy from taking excessively large positions just because the recent window is quiet.

Position sizing and leverage

Position sizing is based on volatility, not raw confidence scores. The core idea is to size contracts so that each position contributes a controlled amount of risk. This improves Sharpe and reduces the chance that a high-confidence but high-volatility signal dominates the portfolio. 

leverage ratio = total risk appetite / risk of one contract

contract leverage ratio = notional exposure per contract / capital

I treated the theoretical Kelly-optimal risk as approximately related to the strategy Sharpe ratio, but used the practical rule that target risk should be closer to half-Kelly. This is more robust because estimation error, tail risk, and regime changes make full-Kelly sizing too aggressive.

Portfolio construction and covariance

The portfolio stage volatility-scales individual instruments and then accounts for correlations across contracts. Long-term covariance estimates are preferred because correlations are noisy and unstable over very short windows. Efficient portfolio optimization is less necessary when leverage is available because portfolio risk can be adjusted through position scaling rather than requiring fully optimized static weights.

A major practical risk is that volatility targeting can create very large positions during quiet periods immediately before crises. Blending volatility and covariance horizons is therefore not just a smoothing choice; it is a crisis-risk control.

Trend overlay

The trend overlay uses MACD-style exponentially weighted moving average signals. My interpretation is that these trend indicators work better for long-position filtering than for aggressive shorting. Instead of using a negative trend signal to force a large short, the more robust use is often to close or reduce long exposure.

Signal strength can be used for sizing, but extreme indicator values need caps or scaling. Capping prevents blow-ups near reversal points, while min-max scaling can preserve relative signal strength when extreme values are not obviously nonsensical. Reversal probabilities could also be incorporated into the sizing logic to reduce account-level damage from sharp reversals.

Empirical observations from the implementation

  • Risk management and position sizing drove performance more than raw signal accuracy.
  • Higher Sharpe is more useful than higher raw return because high-Sharpe strategies can be levered more safely.
  • Annualization of daily volatility assumes low autocorrelation; it is usable but should not be treated mechanically.
  • Equities and commodities often bounce sharply after strong negative trends, while the reverse is less reliable.
  • VIX tends to fall sharply after strong positive trends, while the reverse is less reliable.
  • Initial weights or no-trade warmup periods are important before lookback windows are populated, otherwise early trades can be oversized.

Backtest results

I evaluated multiple strategy variants and volatility targets. The strongest 20-year comparison was between a higher-volatility and lower-volatility target after transaction costs of 0.05% per trade.

Volatility Target Total Return CAGR Volatility Sharpe Max Drawdown Trades
Higher target 19.69× 15.93% 21.91% 0.784 -51.5% 2,549
Lower target 6.38× 10.24% 14.15% 0.760 -38.0% 1,632

The key interpretation is that higher risk targets raised CAGR and total return, but also increased drawdown depth and trading frequency. The Sharpe ratio stayed broadly similar across the targets, suggesting that the trade-off was mainly risk appetite and leverage, not a radically different source of alpha.

Regime behavior

The strategy tended to perform better during post-shock volatility normalization than during long quiet trends. Rolling Sharpe peaks clustered after 2008–09, 2011, and the post-COVID period. The likely mechanism is that volatility targeting stabilizes risk when volatility is high but falling, while the trend overlay avoids some whipsaw losses and re-engages as structure returns.

This suggests the strategy is best suited for volatility normalization and trend resumption regimes. In extended calm periods, risk targeting must be monitored carefully because quiet markets can encourage large positions right before stress events.

MACD mask diagnostic

To test whether the MACD trend mask added genuine incremental performance, I regressed the MACD-overlay monthly returns against the baseline volatility-sizing strategy. The estimated monthly alpha was about 0.0504%, or roughly 0.61% annualized, with t-statistic 1.24 and p-value 0.2134.

The beta was approximately 0.98 and R² about 0.988, meaning the overlay largely tracked the baseline. I therefore interpret the MACD mask as a marginal risk-control adjustment rather than a strong standalone alpha source.

Strategy selection lessons

Selecting a strategy because it won a backtest is sample fitting. I treated the more robust approach as starting with a blended strategy, then gradually reducing weight to components that are inefficient or not economically explainable.

If a result cannot be explained logically and financially, it is likely data mining even if it looks statistically significant. To test whether an overlay adds real value, it should be regressed against the baseline strategy to estimate incremental alpha rather than judged only by standalone backtest performance.

Recommendations

  • Use target volatility in the 10–16% range if the goal is to keep drawdowns below roughly 40%.
  • Use adaptive targets carefully: higher after volatility shocks, lower during extended calm periods.
  • Maintain blended volatility and covariance estimates to avoid crisis-period overexposure.
  • Track performance by regime, not only full-sample performance.
  • Delay trading or preserve initial weights until lookback windows are properly populated.
