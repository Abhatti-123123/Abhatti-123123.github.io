The Most Important Variable Was Never Measured

5 min read

In applied data science, the most important variable is often not directly observed.

A recent willingness-to-pay project made this problem concrete. The goal was to model whether people were willing to financially support conservation efforts for a Canadian estuary. The survey included standard variables such as income, age, education, household characteristics, and location.

A direct model could use these variables as predictors. But this misses something important: people do not make environmental payment decisions from demographics alone.

The missing variable was not simply whether respondents cared about conservation. Some respondents may support the environmental cause but still report a lower willingness-to-pay because they do not trust the government to use the funds effectively.

In that case, the observed payment amount mixes two different objects: valuation of the conservation outcome and trust in the institution implementing it.

A low stated willingness-to-pay does not necessarily mean low environmental concern. It may reflect skepticism about whether the money will be used properly.

Observed answers as noisy measurements

The key modeling idea is to treat survey responses as noisy measurements of an underlying latent state.

Responses about trust, environmental priorities, and beliefs about public responsibility may all partially reveal a respondent's attitude toward government-led conservation. None of them is perfect alone, but together they can help estimate a hidden variable.

Once estimated, this latent attitude measure can be included in the willingness-to-pay model alongside demographics.

Why this matters

Without the latent variable, the model is forced to explain willingness-to-pay using only visible attributes. That can make the model look as if income, age, or education are doing more explanatory work than they really are.

With the latent variable, the model better reflects the actual decision process: willingness-to-pay depends not only on financial capacity, but also on beliefs, trust, priorities, and perceived institutional credibility.

In this project, adding a latent attitudinal component improved explanatory power and produced a more interpretable model.

The broader lesson

This issue appears far beyond environmental economics.

Credit models try to infer borrower quality from observed behavior. Recommendation systems infer preferences from clicks and purchases. Financial models infer information from trades and prices.

In each case, the quantity of interest is hidden. What we observe are indirect traces.

A common mistake in applied modeling is to assume that the variables in the dataset are the variables that matter. Often, they are only proxies.

Sometimes the most important variable in the model is the one that never appeared in the dataset.
