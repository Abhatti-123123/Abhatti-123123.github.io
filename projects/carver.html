Carver Futures Strategy Replication

A systematic futures implementation inspired by Robert Carver's framework, focused on volatility targeting, diversification, risk parity, and trend-following overlays.

Objective

The project implements a diversified systematic futures strategy across equities, bonds, and commodities. The goal was not just to maximize return, but to understand how volatility estimation, position sizing, diversification, and trend filters affect Sharpe, drawdowns, and tail behavior.

Core implementation

  • Back-adjusted continuous futures using the Panama method to avoid treating roll gaps, dividends, and interest effects as trading losses.
  • Volatility-scaled positions using EWMA volatility estimates rather than equal-weighted rolling windows.
  • Blended short- and long-horizon volatility/covariance estimates to avoid overreacting to short-term spikes.
  • Portfolio-level risk parity and leverage based on total risk appetite versus single-contract risk.
  • MACD-based trend overlay used primarily to reduce or close long exposure rather than force aggressive shorts.

Key reasoning

The main lesson was that risk management and sizing mattered more than raw signal accuracy. EWMA volatility avoids artificial jumps caused when a large old return drops out of an equal-weighted window. Short-term volatility clusters, but longer-run volatility mean reverts, so a blended volatility estimate gives smoother sizing across regimes.

I also treated high Sharpe as more important than high raw return because a higher-Sharpe strategy can be levered more safely. Signal strength sizing can help, but extreme indicator values need caps or scaling to prevent large positions near reversal points.

Trend overlay

The trend module uses exponentially weighted moving averages to construct a MACD-style signal. Positive trend values indicate upward trend, negative values indicate downward trend, and the mask can be multiplied into raw position weights to reduce exposure in contra-trend regimes.

20-year backtest results

Target Total Return CAGR Volatility Sharpe Max DD Trades
Higher vol target 19.69× 15.93% 21.91% 0.784 -51.5% 2,549
Lower vol target 6.38× 10.24% 14.15% 0.760 -38.0% 1,632

Interpretation

Higher volatility targets increased CAGR and total return, but also increased drawdowns and trading frequency. The Sharpe ratio stayed broadly stable across targets, suggesting that the main trade-off was leverage and risk tolerance rather than a radically different source of alpha.

MACD mask diagnostic

A regression of the MACD overlay's monthly returns against the baseline volatility-sizing strategy found monthly alpha of roughly 0.0504%, annualized to about 0.61%, with a t-statistic of 1.24 and p-value of 0.2134. The beta was about 0.98 and R² about 0.988, implying the overlay mostly tracked the volatility-sizing baseline. I therefore treat the MACD mask as a marginal risk-control adjustment rather than a strong standalone alpha source.

Risk diagnostics

  • Return distribution was fat-tailed, with excess kurtosis around 7.23.
  • Daily skewness was mildly positive at about 0.255.
  • Annualized Sortino was about 1.128, higher than Sharpe, suggesting downside risk was better controlled than total volatility alone suggests.
  • Hit rate was about 54%, with average up and down days of similar magnitude.
  • Jarque-Bera rejected normality, so tail metrics are more informative than assuming Gaussian returns.

What I learned

The strongest takeaway was that systematic trading performance depends heavily on stable risk estimation, position sizing, and regime-aware interpretation. Selecting a strategy purely because it won a backtest is dangerous; the result needs a financial explanation. If an improvement cannot be explained economically, it is likely data mining even if the backtest looks statistically attractive.
